1 · Taiwan market: small-cap whipsaw vs. the leading rotation

The host's read: small caps strengthened into the Taiwan close but the chart shape looks bad; passives (large-cap names like Yageo and Walsin Tech that institutional money can actually fit into) are correcting, though still above their quarterly line; power components and CCL are holding above their 5-/10-day moving averages — the market's "new leadership." He attributes the pullback to deleveraging, drawing a parallel to Korea's highly-levered speculative culture rather than any fundamental negative.

📊 Passives correcting but holding the quarterly line — the skeleton holds up: Yageo closed 7/8 at NT$891, down −21.8% over 5 days (from a 7/1 high of 1140) but still well above its 60-day line at 663. Walsin Tech closed at 474, down −21.4% over 5 days, versus a ~335 quarterly line — also above it. TSMC held above both its 5- and 10-day lines, down just 1.6% over 5 days — relatively resilient.
⚠️ "CCL is all still strong" had already diverged by recording time: Elite Material was up +61.6% over the past month, clearly the strongest and holding above its averages; but Nan Ya PCB and Elite Material peers Doosan/TUC had turned to small 5-day losses (−4.0%/−2.7%) — using a single leader to represent the whole group can mislead.
❓ "Power components, hundreds of billions in market cap" named no specific tickers — unverifiable.

3 · Falling token costs → the software/security-stock narrative reversal

The host argues that as AI token costs fall, the pessimistic "SaaS apocalypse" and "AI is taking jobs" narratives are being disproven by market data; he cites his own CrowdStrike holding as an example, saying security stocks are "all up and to the right." He sees PLTR and Cloudflare as technically strong and potential rotation targets — along with Apple/Google — if GPU stocks weaken.

✅ The CrowdStrike claim is directionally right: its 52-week high hit just two days before recording (7/6, $209.5), then pulled back −10.4% over the next two days — "making new highs" itself was true at recording time. Cloudflare holds up too, just −4.6% off its 52-week high.
⚠️ Palantir's "chart still looks pretty good" clearly doesn't match reality: its 52-week high was 2025-11-03, 8 months ago; it's now down −37.9% from that high, in a long-running downtrend — not a technically strong stock.

4 · The long-term holding logic: NVIDIA vs. Tesla vs. SpaceX

The host says he's held NVIDIA since the 2018 graphics-card era through the AI era, calling it "always been the cheap company"; Tesla, by contrast, is explicitly a "faith stock" — because he likes Musk — and he's already sold part of his Tesla position to rotate into SpaceX (private), with the eventual goal of having SpaceX merge back into Tesla as a single position. He describes SpaceX's post-listing daily swings as "equal to Buffett's entire fortune in a single day."

✅ NVIDIA's "cheap" claim is data-backed: trailing PE 30.2x, forward PE 19.6x, valuation percentile just 2.8% — nearly the cheapest it's been in a year.
⚠️ Tesla/SpaceX are framed as "faith stocks" not value stocks, and the numbers back that framing up: TSLA's trailing PE is a steep 360x (forward 306x), valuation percentile 71.2% — extremely expensive. That confirms the "non-fundamental allocation" admission rather than a value entry point. SpaceX (listed 2026-06-12) has been extremely volatile since, down over 34% cumulatively from its post-listing high by 7/8. "A day's swing equals Buffett's whole fortune" is rhetorical exaggeration, but against Buffett's ~$148.9B net worth versus SpaceX's market cap, the order of magnitude is roughly plausible — not a precise figure.

Tesla/SpaceX are explicitly framed by the host himself as "faith stocks," not value investing — the PE data (360x trailing) backs that up. This segment reads more like a personal-life/psychological aside than an investment case.

"CCL is all still strong" had already diverged by recording time (only Elite Material is genuinely strong; Nan Ya PCB and peers weakened) — a reminder that group-level commentary can lean on one leader to represent a whole basket. The power-components claim named no specific tickers and is entirely unverifiable — listeners who want to follow up need to do their own stock-picking research.
