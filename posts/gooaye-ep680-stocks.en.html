1. TSMC's Q2 earnings: numbers all check out, yet the stock still sold off

He states TSMC's Q2 EPS was NT$27.25 and gross margin 67.7%, close to consensus; capex was raised sharply from $52–56bn to $60–64bn, above most expectations; Q3 gross-margin guidance is 65–67% with operating margin 56–58%; full-year USD revenue growth guidance was raised from 30% to 40%. But the ADR dropped the day after the call, and he argues the market couldn't find a real bearish reason — Friday's 2330 in Taiwan fell "6%."

✅✅ The hard numbers all check out line-by-line: Q2 EPS NT$27.25 (Futu ADR basic EPS 136.23÷5=27.25), gross margin 67.72% (gross profit NT$860.3bn ÷ revenue NT$1,270.4bn), capex raised to $60–64bn (70–80% into advanced nodes), Q3 gross-margin guidance midpoint ~66%, full-year USD revenue guidance raised to "slightly above 40%" — all precisely matching the public earnings-call materials.
⚠️ Friday's TW drop was deeper than he said: he said "6%"; FinMind actual: 7/16 close 2470 → 7/17 close 2290, down 7.29%. The ADR fell from 409.74 (7/16) to 398.37 (7/17), down 2.77% (intraday low down 5.77%).
✅ June revenue confirms growth is running even hotter than guidance: FinMind shows TSMC's June 2026 revenue at NT$442.68bn vs. NT$263.71bn a year earlier — +67.9% YoY, well above the 40% full-year guide.

2. What actually drove the pullback: Korea's leveraged-ETF blowup, not a cracking AI narrative

He calls this a "capital-flow-driven massacre," pointing specifically at Korean leveraged ETFs/leveraged capital blowing up first and spilling sentiment into global markets, dragging down valuations broadly; he also pushes back on the "cheap Chinese model" bear case, calling it the same misread as the DeepSeek episode (efficiency gains expand usage, they don't shrink it).

✅ Korea's leveraged-ETF blowup is real, with a clear timeline: 16 single-stock leveraged ETFs tracking Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix only launched in Korea on 2026/5/27; AUM ballooned from ~$3bn to ~$9.1bn within a month. On 6/22 the head of Korea's FSS publicly said he "regretted approving" them; some ETFs fell over 25% the next day, tripping KOSPI circuit breakers. As of 7/13, over 1.2 million leveraged accounts hit margin-call thresholds and 320–360k were force-liquidated; KOSPI is down over 25% from its June high, SK Hynix down nearly 40% from its peak. This event genuinely had the reach to spill into TW/US semiconductor sentiment.
✅ Kimi K3's 2.8-trillion parameter count is accurate: Moonshot AI's Kimi K3, released around this period, has 2.8 trillion parameters and beats Claude on some benchmarks — a different model from the older Kimi K2 (1 trillion parameters).

3. TSMC valuation and position sizing: the 15% defensive trigger, forward PE at 21x

He says the broad market's average PE may have been around 25x at the high, with an expected correction target of 18–20x (12–13x in an extreme case). With over half his own book in TSMC, Friday's drop triggered his self-imposed "shift to defense past a 15% drawdown" rule; going forward he'll lean on index exposure (TAIEX futures/0050) and earnings names over chasing high-beta plays.

⚠️ "25x at the high" is a gut-feel estimate — the actual numbers are more nuanced: Futu shows TSMC's trailing PE at 30.1x (ADR), FinMind shows 30.79x for 2330 common shares — the two sources corroborate each other. But forward PE is only ~21.0x, sitting at the 30.4th percentile of its one-year range (well below the one-year average of 31.8x). By his own framework there's theoretically still room to compress further; but looking at forward PE — which reflects the next 12 months of earnings — 21x isn't expensive for a company guiding >40% full-year revenue growth at 65–68% gross margin.
✅ Analyst targets remain constructive: Futu shows 6 analysts covering US.TSM with an average target of $534 (high $650, low $440), 83.3% Buy and 0% Sell; against the $398.37 spot price that implies +34% average upside. TAIEX has fallen from its 6/22 high of 47,741.51 to a 7/17 close of 42,671.27, a 10.62% pullback, with a single-day drop of 6.47% on 7/17.
✅ The 15% defensive rule is disciplined risk management, a reasonable personal framework rather than universal advice; refusing to go to cash (on the view cash depreciates) means this remains risk-tightening within a still-bullish stance, not a turn bearish.

4. Listener Q&A: Flexium's "unfair beating" vs. MediaTek's target with no numbers behind it

A listener describes Flexium (6206), an IPC/POS maker, as posting record profits this year with software revenue up several-fold, yet the stock has drifted lower — "no share of the gains, full share of the pain." He agrees the fundamentals look solid but cautions that a rising B/B ratio in the IPC industry could reflect component shortages rather than pure demand strength, suggesting a small starter position first. Separately, he relays that "an old-timer I know thinks MediaTek (2454) should be at NT$8,000, 9,000, even 10,000," using it to illustrate "the difference between investing and speculating is whether you can stomach being right on fundamentals while the price is wrong."

✅ Flexium's revenue growth and cheap multiple check out: FinMind shows monthly revenue rising from NT$467m (Feb 2026) to NT$501m (Mar), NT$542m (Apr), NT$641m (May), NT$610m (Jun) — roughly +40–90% YoY; Q1 2026 EPS was NT$2.51 vs. NT$2.27 a year earlier (+10.6% YoY); the stock is down 31% from its 52-week high of NT$172.5 (2025-02-21) to NT$119.0 (2026-07-17); trailing PER is only 16.69x. The specific claim that "software revenue grew several-fold" can't be isolated from total revenue and may be an overstatement of one business line by the listener.
❌ MediaTek's NT$8,000–10,000 target has no numbers behind it: FinMind shows the current price at NT$3,370 (down 19.7% from NT$4,195 just two weeks ago), with trailing PER already at 53.71x (historically MediaTek trades 15–25x). The target implies 2.4–3x from here — if earnings stayed flat, PE would need to reach 130–160x; even assuming earnings doubled, PE would still need to hold at a lofty 65–80x. He himself is only relaying this, not endorsing it, but it's the one clearly unsupported call in the episode and shouldn't be taken at face value.
