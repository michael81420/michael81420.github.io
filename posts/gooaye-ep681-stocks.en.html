1. Market read: the "humanitarian corridor" and capital rotation

He stresses that in this pullback "small-caps have fallen harder than the index," and warns against oversized leverage (noting the new generation of retail treats 2.5x leverage as "standard"). He frames the recent bounce as a "humanitarian corridor" — hitting overhead moving-average supply, a cautious stage rather than the start of a momentum leg. He observes capital leaving small-cap/high-multiple growth names and rotating into TSMC, TAIEX futures, and "old-AI" ODMs with earnings defense and cheap valuations.

✅ "Small-caps down harder than the index" checks out directionally: among the names in this episode, passives (Yageo, Walsin, Holystone) and some substrates are down roughly -26% to -51% over the past month, while large-caps like AMD and Google held up relatively better — consistent with the "rotation out of small-caps into defensive large-caps" narrative.
⚠️ "Humanitarian corridor" is a qualitative read, not a verifiable number: it's a subjective description of sentiment and MA positioning; treat it as a risk-management cue, not a timing signal. He himself says "the bigger the cycle, the deeper the pit you fall into" — meaning he too expects the eventual correction to be proportionally larger.

2. AMD Advancing AI 2026: CPU TAM doubling is real, but valuation is at historic highs

He notes AMD is about to unveil Venice (SP7-platform CPU) at Advancing AI 2026 (7/22–23, San Francisco), citing a GPU:CPU revenue ratio of ~2:3, with CPU supply bottlenecks (advanced-packaging capacity, memory) as one of the market's biggest current constraints; customers include Microsoft, Oracle, and reportedly Anthropic. He mentions "CUDA's moat is being slowly lowered," while adding the caveat that "Jensen is no pushover." Intel's next-gen Diamond Rapids / Clearwater Forest lags AMD's roadmap.

✅ AMD's CPU TAM doubling is real: at its 2025/11 Financial Analyst Day AMD gave a 2030 CPU TAM of $60B, raised to $120B at its 2026/5 Q1 earnings. Advancing AI 2026 at San Francisco's Moscone Center on 7/22–23 with a Lisa Su keynote is also confirmed.
⚠️ But AMD's valuation already prices in nearly every bull case: Futu shows US.AMD at trailing PE 182x, forward PE ~83x, in the 98th valuation percentile (near its most expensive ever), up +125% over three months. The TAM upgrade is a real event, but there's very little room for error at this price — any CPU shipment shortfall could trigger a multiple compression.
❓ "GPU:CPU revenue ratio ~2:3" has no independent source — it's a personal estimate, unverified. "CUDA's moat being lowered" is a market narrative, and he left himself a caveat.

3. Google Frozen v2: the weakest speculation in the episode

He speculates the "Frozen V2" chip rumor is a Marvell-designed TIA (TPU Inference Accelerator), calling it an old theme rehashed and likely already digested by the market. He also self-corrects last episode's narrative that "Moonshot's Kimi is highly efficient and could reduce compute demand" — because Kimi later said it needs to prioritize compute for existing customers (not enough to go around), effectively negating the "more efficient models mean fewer servers" inference.

⚠️ "Frozen v2 = Marvell" contradicts outside reporting: public reporting (2026/7) says Frozen v2 is Google's in-house project burning the Gemini architecture directly into silicon, targeting 6–10x the efficiency of current TPUs and not deploying until ~2028, with no mention of Marvell. Marvell's Google collaboration is a separate memory-processing chip + inference TPU — not the "Frozen" series. He adds "correct me if I'm wrong" — the honesty is commendable, but the conclusion doesn't hold.
✅ The self-correction on Kimi's compute narrative is a plus: he proactively admits last episode's "more efficient model → lower compute demand" inference was contradicted by Kimi's own announcement (not enough compute, prioritizing existing customers) — an honest disclosure.

Lighter balance sheet but still loss-making; down 42–45% over the past month — a high-volatility theme stock. STM has turned profitable with a reasonable ~43x forward PE; NVTS and Wolfspeed remain loss-making, so PE is meaningless for them.

7. Objective commentary (invest-playbook framework)

What holds up

The most valuable part is the capital-rotation observation: "small-caps crushed, capital rotating into TSMC/TAIEX futures/old-AI defensives," "substrate revenue strong," and "passives up big then correcting" all match the actual data — sharp tape reading. AMD's CPU TAM doubling ($60B→$120B) is also a real event, not thin air. His self-correction on the Kimi compute narrative shows rare honesty.

Grade the cycle intensity first, then talk valuation

AMD / AI-compute leader is a structural growth story, but at ~83x forward PE in the 98th valuation percentile it prices in the "CPU-bottleneck windfall" and "agentic AI lifting the CPU:GPU ratio" already — a textbook "narrative true but price has run ahead of it," thin margin of safety.

The three substrate names are a textbook deep commodity cycle: trailing PE of 133–276x reflects a depressed earnings base while monthly revenue is already up +25–40% YoY — exactly the invest-playbook case where "a high trough PE can be the buy point." The catch is the stocks already rallied three months, so the market has front-run the recovery and the downside is asymmetric. Passives are the same deep cycle (his own words: "when the tide comes prices rise together, when it ends they crash"), now at the most dangerous post-spike-then-crash stage.

What to reserve judgment on

"Old-AI ODMs at ~10x" clearly understates the real multiple (trailing 16.5–18.4x): the "earnings defense" logic holds, but "cheap" needs a haircut — these are more "relatively less-expensive growth stocks" than "bottom bargains." "Frozen v2 = Marvell" doesn't survive verification — a reminder that many of Gooaye's "scoop"-style takes are collected market rumors and second-order inference, not first-hand verification; cross-check before acting. Overall his "feel" is sharp but his precision on specific numbers/facts has gaps — which is his own stated philosophy (heavy on story, light on multiples).
